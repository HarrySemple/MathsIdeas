Consider the conservation of particles in an arbitrary volume \(V\text{,}\) enclosed by a surface \(S\text{,}\) within a smooth domain \(\Omega\text{.}\) By the law of conservation, we have that:
\begin{equation} \myatop{\text{Particles in }V}{\text{@ }t+\delta t} = \myatop{\text{Particles in }V}{\text{@ }t} + \myatop{\text{Net particles}}{\text{entering }V} + \myatop{\text{Net particles}}{\text{created in }V}\tag{3.2.1} \end{equation}
Now set
\begin{align*} u(x,t) =\amp\text{ Density of particles} \\ f(x,t) =\amp\text{ Rate of change of particle density from sinks/sources} \\ J(x,t) =\amp\text{ Flux of particles} \end{align*}
By [cross-reference to target(s) "(3.2.1)" missing or not unique], we have
\begin{equation*} \int_{V}u(x,t+\delta t)dV = \int_{V}u(x,t)dV - \delta t\int_{S}J(x,t)\cdot dS + \delta t\int_{V}f(x,t)dV \end{equation*}
Applying the divergence theorem and rearranging gives us
\begin{equation*} \int_{V}\frac{u(x,t+\delta t)-u(x,t)}{\delta t}+\nabla\cdot J(x,t)-f(x,t)dV=0 \end{equation*}
As \(V\) is an arbitrary volume within \(\Omega\text{,}\) we have that the integrand itself is zero. In addition, \(\delta t\) is arbitrary and is now sent to zero, giving us:
\begin{equation} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot J-f=0\tag{3.2.2} \end{equation}
The Conservation Equation.
